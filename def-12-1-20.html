Dividends as Ordinary Income

Does the higher tax rate matter?

If considering a dividend investment approach, one of the thoughts that comes to mind is that dividends are always taxed at ordinary income levels, whereas capital gains receive special, lower tax treatment. So capital gains are preferred because they are taxed at a lower rate, but at what income level does this actually matter?

Sure, according to tax brackets in 2020 single filers tax rate jumps from 12% to 22% at $40,126, however, this is marginal tax rate, not effective tax rate – which shows the rate you are being taxed in totality. Effective tax rate is calculated as Total Tax divided by Taxable Income.

At what income level does ordinary income effective tax rate surpass the long-term capital gains tax rate, making capital gains less expensive?

Tables sourced from Bankrate – 2020 Tax Brackets, 2020 Capital Gains

Using this handy calculator at mortgagecalculator.org we are able to determine our Average Tax Rate, otherwise known as Effective Tax Rate. We find that at $98,900 your Average Tax Rate would reach 15.01%, passing the long-capital gains rate of 15%.

To simplify things,because I’m an optimist,lets assume maximum 401k contributions of $19,500, and an average health insurance cost of $200 per month, or $1,400 per year, a total of $20,900. Adding this $20,900 to our $98,900, we see an estimated annual salary of $119,800 where when receiving dividends they would still be taxed.

Further playing around with the calculator, Average Tax Rate hits 20% at an income level of $191,300, adding 401k contributions and health insurance back gives an annual salary of $212,200. Would paying an additional 5% tax on the dividends make you forego receiving cash dividends now vs waiting for selling shares? Lets leave that up to personal preference.

So if your annual pre-tax salary is $119,800 while contributing max to your 401k it is estimated there is no excessive penalty to receiving dividends as ordinary income, making it comparitively the same as receiving the “preferred” capital gains tax.
