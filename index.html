2nd Car vs Mutual Funds

Download 2nd Car vs Mutual Funds Spreadsheet

A Nissan 370Z as a 2nd car or Vanguard Mutual Funds VFIAX/VGSLX ?

What is it worth to you? Long story short...

  • If you put $36,375 into the car and sold it after 5 years, you would have no second car and $19,486 to show for yourself.
  • If you put $36,375 into VFIAX and sold all shares after 5 years, you would have still have no second car, but have $43,811 to show for yourself, an additional $24,395 – which if instead you kept invested would continue to grow and provide $218 in dividends each quarter.
  • If you put $36,375 into VGSLX and sold all shares after 5 years, you would have still have no second car, but have $41,477 to show for yourself, an additional $21,991 – which if instead you kept invested would continue to grow and provide $372 in dividends each quarter.

To see what I did and to work through the methodology, take a look at the spreadsheet above.

Methodology

Started on the ‘Auto Info’ tab, researching purchase and maintenance costs, model specific depreciation and financing rates.


  • Lowest widely available financing rate of 2.69% used after 20% down payment.
  • Ownership costs from Edmunds.com used. These numbers were cut in half to account for reduced weekend usage, expecting 7,500 miles per year rather than 15,000.
  • Depreciation from Edmunds reduced to 75%, assuming age is 50% and mileage is 50% of depreciation, the mileage would be cut in half only.
  • Generously allowed depreciation to play out over time instead of imposing a 10-20% decrease in value from driving it off the lot. Car Edge depreciation is included, however, was not used for calculations.

For the Mutual Funds information, downloaded historical fund price and dividend data from yahoo finance. On ‘Fund Pivot’ tab found the average monthly share price movement and average dividend yield using the earliest starting date of November 2000 through November 2020 to apply to the the cash used on investments instead of sunk into the car.

Tab ‘Auto vs Fund’ compares the scenarios, where you have the initial cash deposit on the asset of $6,6869 and then continually put the same amount of cash into the asset every month – and then sell all assets at the end of five years. Capital gains tax of 15% was applied to the gain in mutual fund. Dividends were taxed at the ordinary income level of someone earning $85,000/year – where although their marginal tax rate is 22% the actual average income tax rate at that level turns out to only be 14%.

Personal Finance Tracker

For almost 10 years I have been logging every dollar I have spent. Inspired by my lack of money, income and also books like 'The Millionaire Next Door' I decided to get a hold of my finances and see where my cash was flowing. After years and numerous updates, behold, my current personal finance tracker for public use.

Note - I became sick of paying Microsoft an annual subscription for Excel/MS Office Suite, so now I use open source LibreOffice LibreCalc for free, its the best freeware/open source spreadsheet software I have tested. It covers all the basics, and unlike the others, I noticed LibreCalc has searchable filters!

Download 202X Personal Finance Template

How to :

1. Get a Mint account and connect all your accounts. This will help input transactions and get a less involved/precise overview.

2. The first of every month update the balance sheet section. This is so you have a snapshot to compare accounts and your net worth against other months.

3. At minumum, once a week sit down, log into Mint.com and enter what you have spent into the categories. Better if you jump on for a few minutes every couple of days and enter expenses because if you wait until two weeks, it starts to become a time consuming chore and also, remembering exactly what you were buying could be difficult.

4. Record all line items with a tick mark of cash or credit just to make note, the check mark is available using the Webdings font, lowercase a. However, you should use the tracker (and all expenses) as if they are cash. You are legally liable to pay, so you reflect it in your expenses NOW. When you put money on your credit card, act like it is cash, dont confuse yourself with the delayed timing of a credit card bill. The purpose of using a credit card is not to use money you dont have, but to spend money on things you already would, and ALSO to receive cash back rewards or other rewards for spending.

5. Update category budgets in row 68, ex. cell F68 for discretionary, cell L68 for groceries.

6. Month Overview net worth end of month figures are pulled from the following months balance sheet. So once you fill out the next months balance sheet you can go back and see how your net worth has changed.
